Learning How to Learn

The single most important learning skill is the ability to ask the right questions. One of the most important questions is, “What’s the best way to learn?” Meta-learning is the study of learning how to learn, and it’s becoming increasingly important since so few college students know how to study. My goal is to teach students how to learn, and ultimately, how to ask the right questions.

The first step is to clarify our goals and assess the student's grasp of the subject. The main goal is typically to raise a student's grade a certain amount. The assessment will be ongoing, as gaps in knowledge reveal themselves over time. Over the past two years I've become familiar with some of the most common pitfalls, and I'll test for those early on. Almost without fail, difficulty with advanced concepts results from incomplete understanding of more basic mathematical concepts. Math builds progressively, so a good foundation is crucial.

With a clear start and end point, we then identify good study habits to utilize, and bad ones to avoid. I teach students how to break a problem into smaller problems, and how to solve each part. Testing students on the material is the most important step, because it helps clarify misunderstanding before they are graded on them. This process repeats itself with adjustments made as necessary. Over the course of our time together I look forward to getting feedback from students on how I can better help them.

Students must study outside of tutoring. In the past some students have waited to start their homework when we begin our tutoring session. Only by independently attempting challenging problems can someone test their understanding. Tutoring sessions are best spent clarifying concepts that remain unclear after students have already tried to find a solution on their own. I do my best to be a great resource, but education is ultimately a process of self-improvement.

Many people have a fear of math. They’ve had difficulties in the past, so they say they aren’t math people, that their brain doesn’t work that way. If you look at a problem and see gibberish, remember Step 0: Don't panic. We can break down problems into smaller, easier problems, and solve one piece at a time until we arrive at our answer.

Too often we receive a problem and immediately begin to work on it without asking, “What’s the best way to solve this problem?” If we don’t clarify the obvious, we’ll often forget it. Solving any math problem is a three step process.

  1. What's the question? When a problem has multiple steps it’s easy to lose sight of the goal. I’ve seen many students do 90% of a problem and box their answer one step short. We break problems into parts to make them manageable, and as a result we sometimes forget that the answers to those smaller problems aren’t what we’re interested in. Always remember the question.
  2. What's known? This includes any equations related to the problem, as well as all the information in the problem, which could include red herrings. It’s very rare for a problem to be given without enough information to solve it. More often, we forget to include equations that involve already known parts.

    A good rule of thumb: for every unknown variable, we need one unique equation. So if we only have one equation we can only solve for one unknown, we need two unique equations to solve for two unknown variables, and so on.

  3. Does the answer make sense? Once you know all the parts of the puzzle, solving is just moving the pieces around. Helping with this process is where I do the majority of my work. Once you've solved the problem you have to compare it to the question from Step 1, and ask yourself if the answer is reasonable.

To summarize: Clarify what you're solving for. Write down things that are true. Move puzzle pieces around until the picture reveals itself.
